# Revenue Management Metrics Explained

## What is Occupancy Rate?

Occupancy rate is the percentage of a hotel's rooms that are occupied on a given night. It's calculated as rooms occupied divided by total rooms available, times 100. A booking counts toward occupancy for every night of the stay except the checkout night itself — a guest checking out on the 5th doesn't count as occupying a room on the 5th, since that room is available again that day. Cancelled bookings never count toward occupancy, since the room was never actually occupied.

## What is ADR (Average Daily Rate)?

ADR measures the average price actually being achieved per occupied room, on a given day or over a period. It's calculated as total room revenue divided by the number of rooms actually sold (occupied), not divided by total rooms available. A high ADR with low occupancy can mean prices are too high for demand; a low ADR with high occupancy can mean money is being left on the table by pricing too low.

## What is RevPAR (Revenue Per Available Room)?

RevPAR is the single most important combined metric in hotel revenue management, because it captures both pricing and occupancy in one number. It's calculated as total room revenue divided by total rooms available (not just rooms sold) — equivalently, RevPAR = ADR × Occupancy Rate. Two hotels can have very different ADR and occupancy but the same RevPAR: a hotel that's 50% full at ₹10,000 ADR and a hotel that's 100% full at ₹5,000 ADR both generate the same revenue per available room. RevPAR is what actually determines profitability, which is why it's the metric revenue managers care about most, more than ADR or occupancy alone.

## Lead time and length of stay

Lead time is how many days before check-in a booking was made. A short lead time (booking a few days before arrival) typically signals urgent, price-insensitive demand — the classic "last-minute premium" pricing logic. A long lead time (booking a month or more ahead) is typically more price-sensitive and often rewarded with an early-bird discount. Length of stay is simply the number of nights booked, calculated as check-out date minus check-in date.

## Weekend and peak season, as this system defines them

A "weekend" check-in in this system means the guest arrives on a Friday or Saturday — the two nights hotels typically see the highest leisure demand. "Peak season" refers to October through February, the cooler, high-tourism months in the Indian hospitality calendar; June through August (the monsoon) is treated as the low-demand season instead.
